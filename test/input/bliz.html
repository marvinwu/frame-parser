At the height of the Great Depression, President Franklin D. Roosevelt sought to document the country in order to gain support for government programs. Several photographers were commissioned, resulting in over 180,000 photos.

The catalogue has been maintained by the Library of Congress, but it wasn’t until recently that the photos became digitally searchable. The following is a small collection of these never before seen photos.

1. Craigville, Minnesota — September 1937

A lumberjack who was beaten up in a fight and then dropped into a Saloon where he was bandaged up.

The timber workers in Minnesota organized their first successful strike in January 1937 and then again one month after the above photo was taken, in October 1937. For many years the lumberjacks were working extremely long hours, lived in squalor and received little pay.

2. Oklahoma City, Oklahoma — July 1939

A young man drinking from a ‘colored’ water cooler in a bus station in Oklahoma City. Segregation of public facilities was not outlawed on a national level until the Civil Rights Act in 1964 — more than 25 years after this picture was taken.

3. Colleton County, Jacksonboro, South Carolina — December 1938

A family relaxes in their residence while on relief in Jacksonboro, South Carolina.

In addition to the well-known Black Thursday stock market crash, South Carolina suffered many other catastrophes leading up to the Great Depression. The boll weevil destroyed large percentages of cotton crops in the state, where more than half of the workers were employed at the time.

While many farm families lived tough lives in decrepit farm houses like the one pictured above, they were considered by some as better off due to their self-sufficiency when compared to urban residents. In urban areas the declining economy caused textile mills and government offices to lay off many workers, and the state government provided little support to the unemployed.

4. Long Pond, Maine — May 1943

A woodsman worker takes a mid-afternoon lunch break at the Brown Company timber holdings in Long Pond, Maine. The lunch spread consisted of a wide variety of foods including roast pork, beans, hot cakes, biscuits, cookies, pie, milk, tea, coffee, and water.

5. New York City, New York — November 1936

A pair of children playing in the street in New York City during the peak of the Great Depression. This picture was taken on 139th Street and St. Anne’s Avenue in Bronx, New York.

6. Jefferson County, Birmingham, Alabama — July 1936

A farmhand working with a hoe on a Jefferson County farm in the South.

This farm employed a system of agriculture called sharecropping where tenants were allowed to use land to grow crops if they relinquish a large portion of their yield to the landowners. Sharecropping was largely a reaction to the end of slavery and mostly used by poorer farmers who did not own their own farming equipment. In the early 1930s — not long before the above photo was taken — the first sharecropper’s labor union was formed in Alabama.

7. Koochiching County, Littlefork, Minnesota

A baby swings between two trees on an improvised hammock in Littlefork, Minnesota. This picture was taken in a blueberry picker’s camp.

8. Radford, Virginia — December 1940

Young men sleep at a railroad station in Radford, Virginia. They were traveling and did not have anywhere else to stay for the night.

9. Radford, Virginia — December 1940

A construction worker sleeps on a small cot in the basement of a furniture store owned by a man named Mr. Tilly.

Mr. Tilly rented beds to fifty men in the basement of his store in exchange for three dollars and fifty cents per week. Many workers in Radford could not find or afford a place to live, so they resided in boarding houses or rented beds in temporary shared living quarters like the basement of Tilly’s.

10. Elko County, Nevada — March 1940

Saddle makers in Nevada also used their leather to produce other relevant items such as cowboy boots and satchels.

11. Palm Beach County, Florida — January 1939

A truck owned by the Tom Tom Herb Tonic company was converted into living quarters in a trailer camp. This type of home was prevalent during the Great Depression and often referred to as ‘Hoover wagons,’ since many blamed President Herbert Hoover for the economic decline.

12. Belle Glade, Florida — January 1937

Migratory workers retrieve water from a shared source at a work camp in Florida.

As part of President Franklin D. Roosevelt’s ‘New Deal,’ many young men lived in work camps in Florida through a program called the Civilian Conservation Corps. The jobs ranged from cutting down trees to creating national parks and government buildings.

13. Harlingen, Texas — February 1939

A migrant sitting in front of her tent home in Harlingen, Texas. There were a large number of these shantytowns throughout the country during the Great Depression, and they were often referred to as ‘Hoovervilles.’

14. Santa Maria, Texas — February 1939

A young boy working in a field near Santa Maria, Texas. He was a Mexican worker and they were often fed Tortillas and cold coffee for lunch.

15. Harrison County, Texas — March 1939

A young boy is eating a bowl of something they called “milk” but it was actually a mixture of flour and water. This type of food was considered a delicacy by poor sharecroppers in the South. The boy was sick so his mother gave him the mixture in order to make him feel better.

16. Tillamook County, Oregon — October 1941

And old dairy farmer is unloading his milk at the Tillamook cheese plant in Oregon.

17. Tillamook County, Oregon — October 1941

Workers load enormous logs onto a truck in Tillamook County, Oregon. They are using an old machine called a donkey engine, which is a steam-powered winch used to lift large objects.

18. Winton, Minnesota — August 1937

An abandoned saloon in Winton, Minnesota. There were an enormous amount of closed businesses throughout the country during the Great Depression, and abandoned storefronts like this were ubiquitous.

19. New York City, New York — September 1942

A woman gets a manicure while drying her hair at a hairdresser on Eighth Street in New York City called Francois de Paris.

20. New York City, New York — August 1942

Many people wait for trains inside of New York City’s Pennsylvania train station. The station is still in use and bustling today but it is more commonly referred to as Penn Station.

21. New York City, New York — 1938

Children playing on the sidewalk on 61st Street in New York City’s East Side.

Thousands of children were homeless during the Great Depression, and over 15% of all New York City residents were receiving public aid due to large-scale unemployment.

22. Sonoma County, California — January 1942

An old female worker packs eggs into cartons in a Sonoma county egg packing plant. The largest incubators in Sonoma county at the time produced over 1,800,000 chicks yearly.

23. Washington DC — November 1935

A group of boys playing with a pigeon in a Washington D.C. neighborhood.

24. Washington DC — November 1935

Another group of children playing in Washington DC in the mid-1930s. This photo was taken in the backyard of their family home. You can clearly see the horrible conditions they had to live in at the time.

25. Muskogee County, Fort Gibson, Oklahoma — June 1939

A migrant family stops on the side of a road to have lunch, and says a prayer before indulging. The Dust Bowl in the mid-1930s had such extreme dust conditions that it could block out sunlight throughout the day. This led to a huge decline in peoples’ ability to farm in Oklahoma. Many farmers migrated to urban areas or relocated their families to other states in search of work.

26. Boundary County, Idaho — October 1939

A Mennonite farmer standing in front of his ranch in Idaho. He was formerly a wheat farmer in Kansas, but moved to Idaho in order to build a stump ranch, which is a ranch where animals graze on the stumps of trees that have been felled. Due to high wheat prices and new mechanization methods in the 1930s, many wheat farmers had to resort to other types of farming.

27. Scioto County, Ohio — May 1938

The wife of a farmer at Scioto Farms in Ohio tends to the gardens where the chickens graze.

28. Essex, New York — August 1943

This photo was taken at Bear Mountain, which is located in Essex, New York, north of New York City. Taken by Gordon Parks, the photo shows Loretta Gyles shooting a bow during ‘interracial activities’ at Camp Fern Rock, which was run by the Methodist Camp Service.

29. Brooklyn, New York — March 1943

This is a photograph of a poster hanging within a Brooklyn, New York war plant workplace, and it was meant to show how a woman should dress at the war plant. This particular poster depicted how a woman should NOT wear her hair while in the plant, since the long and loose hair would be a constant danger around machinery.

30. Butler, Missouri — November 1939

Many sharecroppers were evicted from their plantations and were forced to fend for themselves. They built small cabins like the one in this photograph out of logs, clay, dirt, and various other found materials.

The evicted sharecroppers and their families would usually band together and form communities.

31. Butler, Missouri — November 1939

This photograph shows one of the stove units in a temporary cabin built by evicted sharecroppers.

In the background, you can see a bit more clearly that these cabins were simply made out of small trees and uneven pieces of timber.

Rapides, Louisiana — December 1940

This picture shows construction workers in a new cafe that opened in Camp Livingston.

Camp Livingston was a U.S. army camp opened in 1940, during World War II. It was actually originally named Camp Tioga, but it was later renamed after Chancellor Robert R. Livingston who negotiated the Louisiana Purchase.

Russell, Alabama — May 1941

These servicemen were from Fort Benning and were passing the time in a general store while on leave.

Russell is located within Phenix City, Alabama, and it was only established eight years before this photo was taken.

Harrison, Mississippi — May 1942

These men were in the process of cutting steel for the production of subchaser submarines.

Shortly before this photo was taken, these men knew nothing about cutting steel or submarine production. One of them was a fishing boat engineer and the other a farmer. When a shipyard opened in Harrison, both men were trained to do the job.

New York, New York — December 1938

Believe it or not, this is a photo of East 12th Street in New York City, 1938.

The group of people are unemployed workers who have fallen on hard times. You can see the Christmas tree in front of the shack, an attempt to add a little joy and festivity to their tough lives.
